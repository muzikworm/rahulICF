Prevention And Cure

Uncredited copying, distribution and use of creative works is fairly common. Here’s what you can do to prevent and counter it.

Key Points

01

Specifying ownership of Intellectual Property Rights (IPR) while making a contract is a great start. For commissioned works, IPR belongs to the contractor, unless a written agreement states otherwise. In case of an infringement, a registered copyright is easier to enforce.

02

While the creator of a work is also its first owner, an agreement allows you to define the extent to which another person can reproduce your work. This includes giving a licensed access.

03

Placing a watermark is a handy tool for putting a definite copyright claim on a work, and can be incorporated easily by anyone. However, you may want to check on the aesthetics of incorporating a watermark over your work.

04

If your work has been replicated illegally, take a picture, and have multiple printouts which should indicate the date on record. This will be helpful in backing your claim against infringement.

05

For online reproduction, begin by finding out the person responsible for the post. Contact the content publisher and site owner, and depending on what you seek, give them time to take the work done or give you credits for it.

06

A more formal approach can be taken by sending a notice. While a legal notice can be drafted and sent via a lawyer, even an individual can send one. While drafting a notice, ensure that you address it to the right person and mention the cause of your action. Give them a reasonable time to act on your concern, and send the notice by post or e-mail.

07

When there is no positive response, get in touch with an IPR lawyer and pursue legal action against the defaulter.
